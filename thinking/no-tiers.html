Thinking / No tiers

Why we don't sell tiers.

March 2026 3 min read By Jamil

Tier sheets exist to de-risk buying — not to improve outcomes. For bespoke work, they actively hurt the buyer.

Most agency pricing looks like a three-column table. Bronze, Silver, Gold. Or Starter, Growth, Scale. The buyer picks the column that fits their budget. Everyone goes home.

This is a nice feeling. It is a terrible way to buy bespoke work.

What tiers actually do

Tiers are a shortcut around a conversation neither side wants to have. The buyer avoids admitting they don't know what they need. The seller avoids asking questions that might disqualify the deal.

The result is that the buyer pays for a pre-packaged quantity of work — messages sent, contacts added, hours delivered — that has no guaranteed relationship to the outcome they want.

If two companies with different buyers, different offers, and different sales cycles pay the same amount for "400 LinkedIn messages," at least one of them is being overcharged.

Usually both.

What happens when you scope to fit

Scoping to fit is slower. It means the first conversation is a real conversation. It means sometimes the answer is "we wouldn't work together." It means the seller can't just hand over a PDF and wait.

What you get in return:

  • The work is sized to the actual outcome, not the package.
  • The scope flexes as the program runs — because nothing on the first call was assumed.
  • The seller is incentivised to disqualify bad fits, because they can't pad them into a tier.
  • You know, going in, that the person on the other side was willing to say no.

The honest version

Tier sheets exist because buyers want to compare. Comparison is a fine instinct for software. It is a broken instinct for services where the outcome depends on the fit.

If you need to put three proposals in a spreadsheet and sort by price, the decision you're making isn't about partnerships. It's about procurement. Good service businesses will opt out of that conversation, because they can't compete on it and shouldn't.

Ascent quotes after the first call. Not because we're trying to hide the number — the number is whatever fits the work. But because the number can't exist before the work is understood, and anyone who gives you one before then is selling you a tier with a fresh coat of paint.
